# Prototype Run Bridge 01

## Purpose

This document formalizes the causal bridge from:

- atomic activation units (minimal closed feedback loops)
- sustained activation
- contextual filter emergence
- higher-order cyclic constraint dynamics

Its goal is to prevent conceptual drift and to establish a structurally precise bridge between:

- mechanism (loop)
- regime (activation)
- constraint (contextual filter)
- cycle (unity–disunity–unity dynamics)
- post-unity restart and scale-limited closure

This bridge now incorporates not only contextual filter emergence, but also:

- re-unification
- post-re-unification hold
- staged restart
- local-domain resolution
- scale-limited closure without mandatory larger-scale fragmentation

---

## Core chain

```
gradient perturbation
→ local coupling
→ minimal closed feedback loop formation
→ sustained activation
→ persistence over time
→ constraint stabilization
→ contextual filter emergence
```

## Layer 1 — Minimal Closed Feedback Loop

**Definition:**  
A minimal closed feedback loop is the smallest topologically closed propagation structure capable of sustaining recirculating activation.

**Necessary conditions:**
- closed topology (no open edges)
- sufficient local connectivity
- recirculation within temporal coherence window

**Non-implications:**
- does not imply persistence over time
- does not imply influence beyond its local structure
- does not imply contextual constraint formation

---

## Layer 2 — Sustained Activation

**Definition:**  
Sustained activation is a regime in which a minimal loop maintains recirculating activity over time within its coherence bounds.

**Necessary conditions:**
- intact minimal loop
- feedback reinforcement within temporal window
- absence of destabilizing interference

**Non-implications:**
- does not imply long-term persistence
- does not imply structural influence on external dynamics
- does not imply contextual filter formation

---

## Layer 3 — Persistent Activation

**Definition:**  
Persistent activation is sustained activation that remains stable over an extended temporal interval relative to local decay timescales.

**Necessary conditions:**
- sustained activation maintained over time
- resistance to small perturbations
- internal phase coherence

**Non-implications:**
- does not guarantee downstream influence
- does not automatically impose constraints on surrounding structure
- does not necessarily generate a contextual filter

---

## Layer 4 — Constraint Stabilization

**Definition:**  
Constraint stabilization occurs when persistent activation begins to selectively shape or restrict the propagation of surrounding gradients.

**Necessary conditions:**
- persistent activation
- interaction with surrounding propagation space
- repeated influence on local flow structure
- structural asymmetry in exposure or coupling
- coherence-integrated persistence of selective bias

**Non-implications:**
- does not require global influence
- does not imply full system reconfiguration
- does not yet constitute a contextual filter
- does not follow from persistence alone
- does not follow from uniform spillover or diffusive influence

---

## Layer 5 — Contextual Filter (CF)

**Definition:**  
A contextual filter is a stabilized constraint structure that biases, redirects, or limits future gradient propagation based on prior activation history.

**Necessary conditions:**
- persistent activation
- stabilized constraint effect on surrounding propagation
- reproducible influence on subsequent dynamics
- selective influence (non-uniform effect on propagation paths)
- sustained structural asymmetry
- coherence-integrated persistence across the stabilization window

**Non-implications:**
- is not the same as the loop that generated it
- does not require continuous active reinforcement (may persist transiently)
- does not imply global dominance
- does not emerge from persistence alone
- does not emerge from transient selectivity alone

---

## Key distinctions

### Loop vs Activation vs Filter

- minimal loop → structural mechanism
- sustained activation → dynamic regime
- contextual filter → emergent constraint

### Local constraint vs Contextual Filter

- local constraint:
  - immediate, proximity-bound influence from active structure
  - may be transient
  - does not require persistence or reproducibility

- contextual filter:
  - stabilized, repeatable constraint
  - requires selective bias
  - shapes future propagation beyond immediate activation

**A contextual filter is therefore:**

> a stabilized and reproducible selective constraint, not merely a local effect of activation

---

## Refined causal interpretation

```
loop enables activation
activation enables persistence
persistence under asymmetry enables selective constraint
selective, reproducible constraint enables contextual filtering
```

## Toward quantification

The transitions in this chain suggest measurable dimensions:
-	loop integrity (closure + connectivity)
-	activation persistence (time relative to decay scale)
-	asymmetry strength
-	constraint strength (degree of propagation bias)
-	selectivity (differential influence across paths)
-	reproducibility (stability of constraint across cycles)
-	coherence-integrated persistence

These dimensions form the basis for future quantitative modeling.

# Extension — Cyclic Dynamics (Unity–Disunity–Unity)

The system does not terminate at unified structure.

The extended causal chain includes cyclic behavior:

```
unified_higher_order_filter
→ perturbation or internal tension
→ internal differentiation
→ boundary formation
→ emergence of new contextual filters
→ contextual_filter_interaction
→ coupling / conflict / organization
→ synchronization / shared bias formation
→ re-unification
→ unified hold
→ staged restart
```

## Cycle trigger condition

Transition from unity to differentiation occurs when:
-	internal reconfiguration exceeds local containment capacity
-	or coherence redistribution becomes system-wide

This produces:
-	boundary formation
-	emergence of new contextual filters

The reverse transition (toward unity) occurs when:
-	interactions stabilize into shared constraint structures
-	coherence becomes globally aligned
-	residual coupling amplifies into synchronization and joint bias formation

---

## Key insight

Unified constraint structures are not terminal.

They remain susceptible to:
-	internal tension
-	external perturbation
-	coherence redistribution

This establishes:

> unity is a dynamically stable phase, not a final state.

---

## Structural implication

The system evolves through:

```
constraint formation
→ interaction
→ unification
→ differentiation
→ reformation
```

rather than a linear progression.

This establishes:

> constraint systems evolve through recurring Unity–Disunity transitions driven by coherence redistribution.

---

## Extension — Re-Unification and Return Dynamics

The return path from disunity to unity is not merely the reverse of fragmentation.

Observed return dynamics show that contextual filters can pass through intermediate stages before complete re-unification.

### Return chain

```
fragmented_contextual_filters
→ residual coupling
→ partial synchronization
→ joint bias field
→ complete re-unification
→ unified hold
```

### Return-phase insight

Re-unification:
-	can be complete rather than partial
-	can dissolve prior boundary structure
-	can eliminate visible identity separation
-	need not preserve dual-domain form after integration

This establishes:

> re-unification is a real completion process, not merely a temporary overlap state.

---

## Extension — Post-Re-Unification Hold

After complete re-unification, the system need not fragment immediately again.

A genuine unified hold interval can occur in which:
-	global coherence remains high
-	boundary structure remains absent
-	identity separation remains absent
-	variance may exist only in diffuse, non-organized form

### Hold chain

```
complete_re-unification
→ unified baseline hold
→ diffuse variance or quiet persistence
→ either renewed staged restart
  or continued stable hold
```

This establishes:

> cycle completion and restart onset must be analytically separated.

---

## Extension — Falsification of False Unity

A re-unified state is not falsified merely because later variance appears.

To overturn a genuine re-unification claim, one must observe:
-	direct reinstatement of prior domains
-	rapid boundary return without staged precursor formation
-	asymmetric recovery of older structure inconsistent with fresh differentiation

### Falsification distinction
-	not sufficient to falsify unity:
-	diffuse variance
-	low-grade local fluctuation
-	slow staged restart
-	sufficient candidates for falsification:
-	direct old-domain return
-	immediate boundary reappearance
-	latent dual structure becoming explicitly recoverable without staging

This establishes:

> post-unity fluctuation is not equivalent to failed re-unification.

---

## Extension — Staged Restart After Unity

Restart after re-unification can occur through staged local organization rather than through immediate global fragmentation.

### Restart chain

```
diffuse_variance
→ local seed activity
→ seed amplification
→ proto-boundary pressure
→ repeatable local partition
→ stabilized local-domain entry
→ local-domain consolidation
```

This establishes:

> restart after unity can proceed through lawful local stages before any broader partitioning appears.

---

## Extension — Scale-Limited Closure

A restarted system need not always continue toward larger-scale fragmentation.

The system may resolve into a stable local-domain resting structure while broader partition probes remain weak, plateau, or fade.

### Local closure chain

```
staged_restart
→ local-domain entry
→ local-domain consolidation
→ bounded broader-linkage probing
→ fading residual linkage
→ local-domain scale closure
```

### Scale-limited closure insight

A system can simultaneously exhibit:
-	stable local-domain structure
-	high global coherence
-	absence of system-level separation
-	negligible residual edge-linkage traces

This establishes:

> closure at one scale does not require total elimination of all residual traces from adjacent scales.
