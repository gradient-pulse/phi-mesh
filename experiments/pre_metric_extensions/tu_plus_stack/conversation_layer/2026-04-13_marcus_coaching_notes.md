Opening for Willem and Erik

“Over the past period I have developed something that has become much more concrete than an idea or paper. It is a licensable protocol for LLM-based systems — not a new chatbot or foundation model, but a coordination layer that sits around existing models.

Its purpose is to improve four things that current AI systems still handle weakly in many settings: continuity over time, recovery after disturbance, ambiguity handling before premature closure, and interpretable decision quality under uncertainty.

What makes it interesting to me commercially is that it looks less like an app and more like licensable infrastructure: a protocol grammar that could be embedded into existing AI stacks, especially where systems have to deal with novelty, uncertainty, and repeated stateful interaction.”

Then stop.

That is the key discipline: stop there.

Let them ask.

If they lean in, then go to:

“It works through a strict triadic role structure around a base LLM — structural mapping, comparative continuation, and contextual control — with bounded information flow and recursive feedback. That is what gives it stronger continuity and recovery behavior.”

If they ask “where is this useful first?”, then answer:

“My best current guesses are enterprise decision copilots, long-horizon orchestration, robotics or autonomy supervisory layers, and industrial anomaly-handling systems.”

If they ask “why is this different from normal orchestration?”, then answer:

“Most current systems are still relatively linear in how they coordinate outputs. This protocol is designed around bounded roleholding and feedback-mediated state update, so it handles continuity and unresolved signals differently.”

That is the surface.

The bottom remains there. But you do not start at the bottom.

The connection layer

Try something like this:

“Everyone using current AI seriously runs into the same limitation sooner or later: it can be impressive in the moment, but it often does not really hold continuity the way you need. It loses track of what matters, it flattens ambiguity too quickly, it drifts, and after a disturbance it often behaves as if it is starting fresh rather than recovering intelligently.”

That is immediately legible.

Then:

“What I have been working on is not a new model, but a protocol layer around existing models designed precisely for that weakness: better continuity, better recovery, better handling of uncertainty before premature closure.”

That is the click.

Their own experience, translated

The kinds of everyday hooks that work are:
	•	“AI often sounds fluent, but does not really hold the thread.”
	•	“It can answer, but not always stay oriented in time.”
	•	“It often forgets what the real objective was.”
	•	“It tends to collapse uncertainty into a neat answer too early.”
	•	“When conditions change, it often does not recover so much as reset.”
	•	“It is strong at generation, weaker at stateful coordination.”

That is language they can feel.

A good spoken version

Here is a more natural opening:

“The practical weakness I keep seeing in current AI is not that it cannot generate impressive outputs. It can. The weakness is that it often does not hold continuity very well. It loses the thread, it forgets what matters, it overcommits too early when things are still ambiguous, and when something changes it often resets rather than recovers intelligently.

What I have developed is a protocol layer around existing LLMs aimed exactly at that problem: improving continuity, recovery, ambiguity handling, and state-sensitive decision quality.”

Then pause.

That is much better than jumping straight into mechanism.

If they ask “how?”

Only then say:

“It does that by structuring the model into three constrained roles and feeding outputs back into the next state in a disciplined way, instead of treating each response as a more or less isolated event.”

That is enough. You do not yet need:
	•	structural mapping
	•	comparative continuation
	•	contextual control

unless they are truly leaning in.

“Current AI is often brilliant moment-to-moment, but weak at keeping its bearings.”

“T-Protocol is an attempt to give AI better bearings across time, disturbance, and uncertainty.”

That they can understand immediately.
