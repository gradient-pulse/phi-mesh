# T-Protocol — Multi-Cycle Walkthrough Note v1

## Purpose

This note explains what a multi-cycle T-Protocol demonstration should show.

Its purpose is not yet to provide a full worked implementation trace, but to define the minimum structure of a useful multi-cycle example for licensees and reviewers.

A full multi-cycle worked example may be added later.

---

## Scope

This walkthrough is illustrative, not exhaustive.

It is a compact teaching corridor designed to make the protocol mechanics legible.
It does not attempt to reproduce the much larger bounded testing history through which the protocol was developed and stress-tested across many cycles.

That broader testing history belongs to the substantiation and assessment layers, not to this explanatory note.

---

## Why this note exists

A single cycle shows the protocol mechanics.

A multi-cycle example shows what matters more deeply:

- continuity across time
- mismatch and revision
- ambiguity preservation
- restart without amnesia
- fresh weak recoupling after restart

That is where T-Protocol becomes visibly different from one-shot prompting.

---

## Minimum recommended multi-cycle corridor

A useful multi-cycle example should include at least these phases:

### Cycle 1 — Calm persistence
- one source
- one train
- low fragmentation
- basic continuation expectation

### Cycle 2 — Mild mismatch
- observed return differs slightly from forecast
- mismatch is logged
- mainline remains intact but revised

### Cycle 3 — Ambiguity emergence
- two mutually exclusive continuations become plausible
- branch split occurs
- no forced reconciliation

### Cycle 4 — Resolution and restart
- one branch gains support
- one branch decays
- mainline restarts cleanly without erasing prior contradiction history

### Cycle 5 — Fresh weak recoupling
- new weak relational evidence appears
- recoupling is treated as fresh evidence
- old collapsed joint field is not automatically revived

---

## What such a walkthrough should demonstrate

A strong multi-cycle example should show that the protocol can:

- keep state across cycles
- revise without unnecessary collapse
- preserve contradiction without smoothing
- restart without amnesia
- distinguish fresh evidence from memory-driven revival
- remain answerable to returned traces

---

## What it should not become

A multi-cycle walkthrough should not become:

- a theatrical story
- a prose-heavy metaphor
- a substitute for conformance testing
- an uncontrolled domain-specific detour

It should remain a disciplined operational example.
