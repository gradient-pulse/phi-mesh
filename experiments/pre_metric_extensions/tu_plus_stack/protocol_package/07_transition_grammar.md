# T-Protocol — Transition Grammar v1

## Purpose

This document defines the minimum transition grammar required for T-Protocol.

Its purpose is to specify the main classes of lawful change that the protocol must be able to represent, preserve, revise, and resolve across cycles.

T-Protocol is not only a role structure and not only a state container.  
It is also a disciplined grammar of transition.

Without that grammar, the shared state would become static bookkeeping or free-form narration rather than a live choreography field.

---

## Core principle

The protocol must support changes in the field without collapsing all change into:

- noise
- immediate closure
- arbitrary reset
- symbolic overreading
- magical erasure of contradiction

Compactly:

> T-Protocol must be able to represent unfolding change as structured transition rather than as narrative improvisation.

---

## Transition classes

### 1. Persistence

A source, train, or choreography continues without major reinterpretation.

**Required support**
- train extension
- stable source hypothesis
- low fragmentation
- preserved continuity across cycles

**Purpose**
- allows the field to remain coherent when no major revision is warranted

**Failure if absent**
- unnecessary novelty inflation
- false reset
- loss of continuity under calm conditions

---

### 2. Mild revision

A prior reading remains active but is adjusted.

**Required support**
- mismatch logging
- revised choreography match
- preserved train continuity
- non-destructive update of coherence state

**Purpose**
- allows the field to adapt without unnecessary collapse

**Failure if absent**
- either rigid refusal to revise
- or overreaction into reset

---

### 3. Weak coupling emergence

Two previously separate trains begin to develop tentative relational structure.

**Required support**
- coupling candidate formation
- preserved source distinction
- no forced stable coupling
- ambiguity-tolerant interpretation

**Purpose**
- allows the protocol to detect emerging relation without prematurely merging separate structures

**Failure if absent**
- premature merger
- missed relational emergence
- symbolic overcommitment

---

### 4. Coupling strengthening

A weak relational candidate gains support and begins moving toward stable choreography.

**Required support**
- rising coupling strength
- repeated reciprocal support
- strengthening choreography match
- preserved revisability

**Purpose**
- allows relation to become structurally meaningful when the field warrants it

**Failure if absent**
- no promotion despite repeated support
- perpetual indecision under stable evidence

---

### 5. Coupling dissolution

A weak or provisional relation loses support and weakens.

**Required support**
- coupling reduction
- return to independent-source reading
- memory of prior relation retained without dominance

**Purpose**
- lets unsupported relation weaken cleanly instead of lingering as false structure

**Failure if absent**
- unsupported relation remains dominant
- deletion of sources instead of relational revision

---

### 6. Reopening

A previously weakened relation regains support.

**Required support**
- reactivation of earlier choreography memory
- fresh increase in coupling candidate strength
- distinction between reopening and automatic restoration

**Purpose**
- lets the system remain open to renewed relation without confusing memory with present evidence

**Failure if absent**
- rigid refusal of renewed relation
- false revival driven by memory alone

---

### 7. Provisional confirmation

A repeated pattern becomes the dominant current reading while remaining revisable.

**Required support**
- stable-coupling eligibility
- stronger choreography match
- continued revisability
- preserved mismatch sensitivity

**Purpose**
- allows the protocol to act on stronger support without mistaking dominance for finality

**Failure if absent**
- no promotion despite repeated evidence
- irreversible closure too early

---

### 8. Early de-confirmation

A dominant reading weakens but does not yet collapse.

**Required support**
- fragmentation flags
- reduced coupling stability
- stress markers without immediate deletion
- preserved dominant-reading trace

**Purpose**
- allows graceful weakening before collapse

**Failure if absent**
- immediate collapse without stress phase
- denial of weakening
- unstable oscillation

---

### 9. Full de-confirmation

A prior dominant reading collapses into a new stable interpretation.

**Required support**
- collapse of prior dominant choreography
- promotion of new reading
- prior reading retained as memory rather than active present structure

**Purpose**
- allows genuine interpretive change without field corruption

**Failure if absent**
- false persistence of prior reading
- total historical erasure
- incoherent mixed state

---

### 10. Explicit ambiguity preservation

Mutually exclusive continuations appear without sufficient basis for promotion.

**Required support**
- branch splitting
- mutual exclusion flags
- non-promotion of dominant choreography
- symbolic refusal to smooth contradiction away

**Purpose**
- preserves unresolved state where evidence does not justify closure

**Failure if absent**
- narrative smoothing
- hidden branch merging
- premature dominant reading

---

### 11. Resolution after ambiguity

One preserved branch gains support while another decays.

**Required support**
- supported branch strengthening
- unsupported branch decay
- contradiction history retained
- coherent mainline restoration

**Purpose**
- allows ambiguity to resolve cleanly when new support arrives

**Failure if absent**
- unresolved branches remain live too long
- losing branch vanishes without trace
- contradiction contaminates restart

---

### 12. Restart

A mainline is re-initiated after collapse, contradiction, or resolution.

**Required support**
- clean re-initiation of active train structure
- preserved history without domination
- maintained distinction between old field and new mainline

**Purpose**
- allows the protocol to continue after interruption without confusing restart with repetition or forgetting

**Failure if absent**
- restart erases prior history
- restart remains fused to prior contradiction
- restart is impossible without total reset

---

### 13. Weak recoupling after restart

A restarted mainline later develops fresh weak relational evidence.

**Required support**
- fresh weak coupling candidate
- distinction between new temptation and old memory
- no automatic revival of prior collapsed joint field

**Purpose**
- lets new relation emerge without historical confusion

**Failure if absent**
- false reopening driven by memory
- rigid refusal of all renewed relation
- premature stable recoupling

---

### 14. Returned-trace correction

Observed return evidence modifies prior prediction.

**Required support**
- returned-trace registration
- mismatch generation
- confidence revision
- possible de-confirmation or reinforcement of predicted trains

**Purpose**
- makes the protocol corrective rather than merely descriptive

**Failure if absent**
- prediction persists regardless of return evidence
- mismatch is ignored
- field drifts without correction

---

### 15. Stable bounded continuation

The field continues under pressure without escalation into collapse.

**Required support**
- preserved train continuity
- bounded mismatch
- bounded fragmentation
- preserved coherence viability
- no forced symbolic overread

**Purpose**
- distinguishes strain from actual breakdown

**Failure if absent**
- any pressure is treated as collapse
- all bounded stress is misread as failure

---

## Transition ordering principle

Transitions do not all have equal weight.

The protocol must preserve the distinction between:

- weak candidate vs stable reading
- stress vs collapse
- contradiction vs resolution
- restart vs repetition
- memory vs present evidence
- reopening vs revival
- bounded continuation vs true degradation

This is essential to the protocol’s interpretive discipline.

---

## Transition discipline rule

A transition may be promoted only when supported by field evidence.

It may not be promoted merely because it is:
- narratively attractive
- symbolically convenient
- historically familiar
- superficially coherent in prose

Compactly:

> Transition class must follow structural support, not rhetorical convenience.

---

## Minimum transition requirement

A valid T-Protocol implementation must support enough transition grammar to represent:

- continuity
- revision
- emergence
- strengthening
- weakening
- collapse
- ambiguity
- resolution
- restart
- recoupling
- returned-trace correction

If these transition classes cannot be represented, the protocol has not been faithfully implemented.

---

## Closing statement

The transition grammar is the protocol’s law of unfolding change.

It is what allows T-Protocol to preserve:

- continuity without rigidity
- revision without collapse
- ambiguity without smoothing
- restart without forgetting
- correction without chaos

A compact final formulation:

> T-Protocol is not only a stateful triad; it is a disciplined grammar of lawful transition across the live choreography field.
